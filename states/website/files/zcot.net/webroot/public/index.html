Hello, suspicious person!

If you're reading this, you most likely encountered an email address of the form some.service@zcot.net and wondered if the website exists and if such an address could be legitimate. I assure you, the address you were given is indeed correct, legitimate, and all messages sent to it will be delivered to the intended person.

What is zcot?

zcot is an email forwarder owned by me, a single individual. It accepts messages for virtually any value in the local-part of an email address (the text to the left of the @-sign) and each received message is forwarded to my real, unshared address.

Why was zcot created?

There are quite a few reasons for this, but most of them boil down to a combination of bad experiences with service providers and my own dumb intransigence. Some more specific reasons are as follows:

  • Just as there is benefit in never reusing a password across more than one service, there is also benefit in never reusing a user name—which in many cases is an email address—across more than one service as well. A zcot address for one service is globally unique and not reused on any other service or site.
  • Phishing messages become more obvious, as there is a high likelihood that the fake message would not have been sent to the correct zcot address for the service that purportedly sent it.
  • When receiving unsolicited and unexpected messages, the presence of a zcot address can help determine if the sender is likely to have the business relationship they say they do. As a more concrete example: an unanticipated message claiming that I am entitled to payment in a class action settlement becomes less suspicious if it is sent to a zcot address I used during a prior interaction with the named defendant.
  • Should a zcot address ever show up in a data breach, it will be obvious which service was the source of the data.
  • Should my real address start receiving spam or unwanted marketing materials, the zcot address that forwarded the message will reveal which service sent the material (or which service shared the address with a third party).
  • Using unique and rather meaningless zcot addresses on services thwarts unwanted "find me by email" functionality. It also helps prevent construction of a social graph from other users who may share their contacts list—in almost all cases the shared contacts would not contain any zcot addresses the service has on file for me.
  • As companies merge and acquire one another, often user accounts are unexpectedly linked in ways that are not predictable, reversible, or agreeable. With different zcot addresses at each service, the merged entity cannot reliably link the accounts or correlate my identity.
  • Sometimes it's desirable to create more than one account on a service that requires unique email addresses across all registrations.

Can't the same thing be accomplished with +?

Sure it can. But having tried that approach for several years, I found it insufficient.

For the unaware, many email providers (notably Gmail) allow their users to insert a single plus sign (+) in their local-part and anything after it will be ignored when delivering the messages. So joe+something@example.com, joe+anything@example.com, and joe+writhing@example.com are three seemingly different addresses, but mail sent to each of them will all ultimately go to the same mailbox: joe@example.com.

In theory, this should work great for my use case. In practice, it's disheartening to discover how many services have boneheaded email validation routines that don't consider the plus sign to be a legal character. Without exaggeration, I would say one in five services have some kind of limitation pertaining to the scheme, in some cases resulting in being unable to even log in without contacting customer service to restore access. (Have you ever tried talking a CS representative through the fact that your email address contains a plus sign?) This sort of thing happens with infuriating frequency.

From the standpoint of creating a bulletproof mechanism for ensuring email uniqueness and traceability, the plus sign scheme isn't perfect either. The trick is known well enough among developers and product designers that the plus-sign portion is often stripped off, leaving only the underlying email address. The anonymity, uniqueness, and traceability aspects are all lost when this happens.

If you are a developer, or a person with influence over the design and logic rules of a web application, please listen to me: in an email address, every non-empty string is legal to the left-hand side of the @-sign. I don't care what regular expression you found on a message board post, or what rules are imposed by other competitors' websites, or what your gut thinks is correct. Every value—very short values, very long values, quoted spaces, accented characters, ☔, complicated sequences of combining characters—every value is legal on the left-hand side of the @-sign. The world is a big place, with lots of different writing systems and mail server software. To think that your service is the end-all arbiter of what is legal and illegal in such a vast and complicated world is naive and obstinate.
